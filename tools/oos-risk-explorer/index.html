OOS Risk Explorer

This page helps you understand how bias and precision turn a reportable-result distribution into OOS tail risk.

Scientific question

When a reportable result has bias and variation, how much of its distribution falls outside the specification limits, and which bias-precision combinations still respect the selected OOS target?

Scientific idea

OOS risk is a tail-area problem. Bias moves the distribution center; precision changes its width; specification limits decide how much probability sits outside.

Mentor note

Acceptance criteria are not just isolated cutoffs. They define a boundary: a method can tolerate some bias and some imprecision only while the reportable-result tail area remains small enough for the intended decision.

Accuracy shifts center

A biased method can look precise while sitting closer to one specification limit.

Precision widens spread

A centered method can still create OOS risk when the reportable-result spread is too wide.

OOS is tail area

The shaded tails are the probability that reportable results fall outside the limits.

Interpretation

Formula audit

USP <1033>

Prob(OOS) = Phi(ZL) + 1 - Phi(ZU)

ZL = [ln(LSL) - RBlog] / sigma

ZU = [ln(USL) - RBlog] / sigma

ChP 9401

ChP terminology maps the same tail-area reasoning to ACRB and ACRVV on log scale.

ACRBlog = ln(1 + ACRB / 100)

sigma = ln(1 + ACRVV / 100)

Unified log-scale

Scientific reference

Scope of use

This explorer uses USP <1033> and ChP 9401 terminology to teach a unified log-scale view of reportable-result OOS probability. It is intended for learning and validation design support, not as a substitute for formal statistical review.
